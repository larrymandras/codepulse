## 1. System Classification

**System Type:** Extraction (LLM-as-judge producing rubric-scored structured output; not RAG, not multi-agent, not conversational)

**Description:**
A nightly Convex `internalAction` acts as an LLM judge over sampled Ástríðr agent sessions, scoring observable behavior (task completion, error handling, tool-use efficiency, cost discipline) against a code-defined rubric and writing per-dimension + overall scores (0-1) into an `evalScores` table alongside Ástríðr's own directly-emitted binary `task_quality` scores. The sole consumer is Larry, the single operator of both CodePulse (the dashboard) and Ástríðr (the personal AI assistant framework being evaluated) — there is no external user, customer, or third party in this loop. "Good" means: no score silently drops (EVAL-01's core motivation — scores were previously written to Langfuse only and never reached CodePulse), the judge's numbers are trustworthy enough that a downward trend on the new Quality page means something actually degraded rather than judge noise, and a persona-model/instruction change that quietly hurts quality gets surfaced as a regression alert instead of going unnoticed for weeks.

**Critical Failure Modes:**
1. **Silent score loss** — a `task_quality` event or judge-scored row fails to persist (dropped ingest, failed idempotency write, provider outage swallowed without a retry) and neither the pipeline nor the operator ever finds out; this is the exact failure EVAL-01 exists to close, so regressing to it is the single worst outcome.
2. **Uncalibrated judge treated as ground truth** — the judge model drifts, the rubric wording is ambiguous, or provider/model changes shift the scoring baseline, and the resulting numbers get read as "quality changed" when actually "the ruler changed." With zero human-labeled reference set and one operator doing all calibration, this is the highest-likelihood failure mode in the whole phase.
3. **Regression alert false positive ("crying wolf")** — the before/after window-mean detector fires on ordinary sampling noise (small per-persona nightly sample sizes) rather than a real quality drop, and Larry starts ignoring or muting the Quality-page alert channel the same way he would any other pager blaring on noise (Larry's standing zero-false-positive precision bar, D-14).
4. **Judge digest omits the evidence that would have changed the score** — the 200-event cap, aggregation, and ~200-300 char truncation (D-06/context strategy) strip out the one error or tool-churn signal that actually mattered, producing a confidently-wrong score with a plausible-sounding rationale attached.
5. **Trend line poisoned by an unlabeled rubric/judge-version change** — the rubric wording, dimension set, or judge model/config slot (`intelligence.llm_eval`) changes over time but historical `evalScores` rows carry no version marker, so a later trend read compares scores generated under two different rulers as if they were one continuous series.

## 1b. Domain Context

> Researched by `gsd-domain-researcher`. Grounds the evaluation strategy in domain expert knowledge.

**Industry Vertical:** Developer tooling / AI-agent observability (LLMOps) — internal eval infrastructure for a personal AI assistant framework, not a customer-facing or regulated-industry product.

**User Population:** One person: Larry, acting simultaneously as the sole operator of CodePulse (the dashboard consuming these scores) and the sole builder/maintainer of Ástríðr (the agent framework being scored). No end users, customers, clinicians, or third parties ever see or are affected by these scores.

**Stakes Level:** Low-to-Medium. There is no third-party harm surface, no regulatory exposure, and no revenue-facing decision gated on a single score. The realistic downside of a wrong output is wasted triage time (chasing a false-positive regression alert) or a missed real regression in a personal assistant's behavior — inconvenient, not dangerous.

**Output Consequence:** A judge score or regression alert feeds exactly one decision loop: Larry deciding whether to investigate/roll back a recent persona config or model change. Nothing downstream is automated on top of these scores (no auto-rollback, no auto-throttling) — a bad score at worst prompts a look, never a silent system action.

### What Domain Experts Evaluate Against

```
Dimension: Judge calibration against human judgment
Good: The judge's scores agree with what Larry would score by hand on a small spot-check sample (target correlation/agreement, not just "looks reasonable")
Bad: The judge is trusted and acted on (e.g., an alert fires) with zero human spot-check ever having been done to sanity-check it
Stakes: Critical
Source: LLM-as-judge production practice (2026) — a judge is only "production-ready" once it has a locked rubric and a measured agreement rate against human labels; without that, per ai-evals.md, "LLM judges produce noise rather than signal"
```

```
Dimension: Rubric anchoring, not vague dimension names
Good: Each rubric dimension (task completion, error handling, tool efficiency, cost discipline) has a written definition plus at least one good-example and one bad-example digest so the model has an anchor, not just a label to guess at
Bad: The system prompt asks the judge to score "efficiency" or "quality" with no anchor examples and no definition of what a 0.3 vs 0.8 looks like
Stakes: High
Source: ai-evals.md rubric design section — "Generic metrics are meaningless without context... a rubric must define what scores X and Y look like"; this phase's own D-09/4b.3 already commit to 1-2 inline few-shot anchors per dimension
```

```
Dimension: Judge-version / rubric-version stability of trend data
Good: Every stored score is attributable to a specific rubric wording + judge model/config version, so a trend line never silently splices two different measurement instruments together
Bad: The rubric prompt or `intelligence.llm_eval` model is changed and historical scores are read as continuous with new ones, with no marker distinguishing "before" from "after" the ruler changed
Stakes: High
Source: Practitioner consensus on eval trend integrity (Langfuse/Braintrust-style tooling convention of versioning prompts/rubrics) — an unversioned rubric is a known cause of phantom trend breaks in production eval pipelines
```

```
Dimension: Regression-alert precision (false-positive discipline)
Good: An alert fires only when the before/after sample sizes are large enough (≥5 judged sessions/side per D-14) and the drop clears a threshold wide enough to rule out ordinary night-to-night sampling noise at ~3 sessions/persona/night
Bad: An alert fires off a 2-vs-2 session comparison where a single unusually bad session swings the mean, training the operator to distrust or mute the channel
Stakes: Critical (operational trust, not safety) — this is the one dimension Larry has already named explicitly (D-14, "zero-false-positive precision bar")
Source: Larry's standing project convention (CLAUDE.md "Code Review & Findings Precision" — optimize for zero false positives, not maximum coverage) applied to alerting; also general production-monitoring guidance in ai-evals.md ("watch for signal-metric divergence")
```

```
Dimension: Digest fidelity — judging on the evidence that matters
Good: The per-session digest handed to the judge (capped events, aggregated tool/error counts, truncated free-text, cost/token summary stats) still contains whatever actually drove a bad outcome — e.g., a truncated 200-char error snippet still names the failing tool/operation
Bad: A session fails badly for a reason that got aggregated away or truncated out of the digest, and the judge scores it "fine" with a plausible but evidence-free rationale
Stakes: Medium
Source: This phase's own D-06/Context Window Strategy design constraint (no transcript access, digest-only judging) — a domain-specific version of the general "judge sees only what you show it" failure mode
```

### Known Failure Modes in This Domain

- **Silent pipeline breakage (the EVAL-01 motivation).** Telemetry/eval pipelines fail silently far more often than loudly — a dropped ingest event, a swallowed provider 5xx, or a permanently-poisoned idempotency key produces "no data" that looks identical to "nothing happened" rather than an error anyone notices. This is precisely why EVAL-01 exists (scores were being written to Langfuse only, never reaching CodePulse) and why 4b.1/4b.2 explicitly forbid inserting a partial/zeroed row on judge failure — a missing row must stay legitimately re-sampleable, not silently look like a real "fine" score.
- **Uncalibrated judge noise mistaken for signal.** Without a human-labeled reference set (there is none planned here — one operator, no dedicated labeling budget), there is no mechanical way to know if the judge's 0.72 vs 0.81 on two sessions reflects a real quality difference or model-level scoring jitter. The honest mitigation available at this scale is `temperature: 0` (already chosen) plus periodic ad hoc spot-checks by Larry reading a judge rationale against the actual session — not a formal calibration study.
- **Regression alerts crying wolf on small-sample noise.** At ~3 sampled sessions/persona/night, night-to-night score variance is naturally high; a naive threshold-on-any-drop detector would fire constantly. D-12/D-14's minimum-session-count-per-side + conservative-threshold design is a direct response to this — the domain research confirms this is the single highest-risk failure mode for this kind of low-volume nightly judge system, not a hypothetical.
- **Trend lines poisoned by an unversioned rubric or judge-model change.** If the judge's system prompt, dimension set, or `intelligence.llm_eval` model is ever changed, every score after that point is being generated by a different measuring instrument. Without a version marker on stored rows, a future "quality dropped after persona change X" read could actually be "quality dropped after *the judge* changed" — indistinguishable from the data alone.

### Regulatory / Compliance Context

None identified for this deployment context. This is single-operator internal tooling with no customers, patients, or regulated data subjects in the loop. ## 2. Framework Decision

**Selected Framework:** Direct provider API calls (raw `fetch`, no SDK) — extend `convex/briefings.ts`'s dual-provider caller pattern (anthropic `x-api-key` / openai Bearer) into a dedicated eval config slot

**Version:** No SDK to pin. Anthropic Messages API `anthropic-version: 2023-06-01`, judge model `claude-haiku-4-5` (config-slot default, swappable). Optional dev-time dep: `zod` (latest 3.x) for judge-output validation.

**Rationale:**
Convex `internalAction`s are a constrained serverless TS/fetch runtime (D-06) that cannot run Python frameworks and gains nothing from a Node orchestration SDK for a single structured judge call per session. D-07 already mandates reusing the proven `briefings.ts` fetch pattern in an isolated config slot (`intelligence.llm_eval`) so judge scoring never silently drifts when other LLM surfaces change models. `package.json` confirms zero existing AI-framework dependencies — the codebase's own convention is raw fetch, not SDK-mediated calls. This is the "direct SDK call beats framework for simple calls" anti-pattern rule applied consciously.

**Alternatives Considered:**

| Framework | Ruled Out Because |
|-----------|------------------|
| Vercel AI SDK (`ai` + `generateObject`/zod) | Kept as the lightest upgrade path if raw-fetch JSON parsing of rubric output proves brittle — not needed for a single structured judge call; adds a dependency the codebase doesn't have |
| LangChain / LlamaIndex / CrewAI / LangGraph / Google ADK / AutoGen / Haystack | Python-first or heavy-runtime orchestration frameworks — cannot run (or gain nothing) inside a Convex serverless `internalAction`; this is one structured call per session, not orchestration/RAG/multi-agent |

**Vendor Lock-In Accepted:** No — the dual-provider caller (anthropic `x-api-key` / openai Bearer) plus the shared `JUDGE_TOOL.input_schema` → `response_format.json_schema` translation keeps the judge model-agnostic; switching provider is a config-slot change, not a code change. Caveat: a provider/model swap changes the measuring instrument — version stamping (E5) makes that visible in trends rather than preventing it.

### Entry Point Pattern
```typescript
// Minimal working shape of the nightly judge call for ONE sampled session.
// This is the Anthropic branch of a dual-provider caller (see Section 4 for
// the full callJudgeLLM() with OpenAI fallback and retry loop).

const JUDGE_TOOL_NAME = "score_session";

const JUDGE_TOOL = {
  name: JUDGE_TOOL_NAME,
  description:
    "Records rubric scores (0-1) for one Astridr agent session based on " +
    "observable behavior: task completion, error handling, tool-use " +
    "efficiency, and cost discipline. Each dimension score is a float in " +
    "[0,1]. Include a one-sentence rationale per dimension.",
  input_schema: {
    type: "object",
    properties: {
      task_completion: { type: "number", minimum: 0, maximum: 1 },
      task_completion_rationale: { type: "string" },
      error_handling: { type: "number", minimum: 0, maximum: 1 },
      error_handling_rationale: { type: "string" },
      tool_efficiency: { type: "number", minimum: 0, maximum: 1 },
      tool_efficiency_rationale: { type: "string" },
      cost_discipline: { type: "number", minimum: 0, maximum: 1 },
      cost_discipline_rationale: { type: "string" },
      overall: { type: "number", minimum: 0, maximum: 1 },
    },
    required: [
      "task_completion", "task_completion_rationale",
      "error_handling", "error_handling_rationale",
      "tool_efficiency", "tool_efficiency_rationale",
      "cost_discipline", "cost_discipline_rationale",
      "overall",
    ],
  },
} as const;

async function callAnthropicJudge(
  apiKey: string,
  model: string, // "claude-haiku-4-5"
  systemPrompt: string,
  userPrompt: string
) {
  const resp = await fetch("https://api.anthropic.com/v1/messages", {
    method: "POST",
    headers: {
      "Content-Type": "application/json",
      "x-api-key": apiKey,
      "anthropic-version": "2023-06-01",
      // No thinking/budget_tokens config — Opus 4.8+/Claude 5 SDKs reject it,
      // and forced tool_choice is incompatible with extended thinking anyway.
    },
    body: JSON.stringify({
      model,
      max_tokens: 1024,
      temperature: 0, // deterministic judging, not creative generation
      system: systemPrompt,
      messages: [{ role: "user", content: userPrompt }],
      tools: [JUDGE_TOOL],
      // Force the judge to always emit the scoring tool call — no
      // preamble text, no "let me think about this" chatter to parse around.
      tool_choice: { type: "tool", name: JUDGE_TOOL_NAME },
    }),
  });
  if (!resp.ok) {
    throw new Error(`Anthropic judge error ${resp.status}: ${await resp.text()}`);
  }
  const json = await resp.json();
  const toolUse = json.content?.find((b: any) => b.type === "tool_use");
  if (!toolUse) throw new Error("Judge response contained no tool_use block");
  return toolUse.input; // already-parsed JSON object matching input_schema
}
```

### Key Abstractions
| Concept | What It Is | When You Use It |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Forced tool use (`tool_choice: {"type": "tool", "name": ...}`) | Tells Claude it must call this exact tool; suppresses free-text preamble | Every judge call — this *is* the structured-output mechanism for the Anthropic branch (no SDK `.parse()`/beta header needed) |
| `input_schema` on the tool | JSON Schema Claude's `tool_use.input` is generated against | Defines the rubric shape once; reused for both the Anthropic tool call and (translated) the OpenAI `response_format.json_schema` |
| `internalAction` | Convex's non-transactional, side-effecting function type — the only one allowed to `fetch()` | The judge call itself (network I/O); never put `fetch` in a mutation/query |
| `internalQuery` / `internalMutation` | Convex functions callable only from other Convex functions (not HTTP/client) | Reading session/events/llmMetrics data for the prompt digest; writing the `evalScores` row |
| `agentConfigs` config slot (`intelligence.llm_eval`) | Existing key-value config table pattern from `briefings.ts`, one row per named slot | Storing the judge's own provider/model/apiKey, separate from `llm_primary`/`llm_backup` so briefing-model changes never silently reweight scoring (D-07) |

### Common Pitfalls
1. **`tool_choice: {"type": "tool", ...}` is rejected when extended thinking is enabled.** Not a risk here since no `budget_tokens`/thinking config is used, but if a future refactor ever adds thinking to this call path, forced tool use will start returning 400s — the two are mutually exclusive on the Anthropic Messages API.
2. **Convex `internalAction` does not auto-retry on throw.** Unlike a scheduled mutation inside Convex's own transactional retry envelope, an action that throws (429, 5xx, network blip) just fails and logs — nothing re-runs it. The judge loop must implement its own bounded retry (see 4b.1), or one transient provider hiccup silently drops that session's score for the night.
3. **OpenAI strict `response_format.json_schema` treats "optional" fields differently than Pydantic/zod intuition.** Strict mode requires every property in `required`; an "optional" rationale field must be typed `["string", "null"]`, not simply omitted from `required` — porting the same nullable-dimension schema to the OpenAI fallback branch needs this adjustment or the API 400s.
4. **Convex prompt-cache invalidation on `tool_choice` changes.** If the judge system prompt is ever moved behind Anthropic prompt caching (recommended in 4b.5 since it's static per night), changing `tool_choice` between calls invalidates the cached blocks. Keep `tool_choice` constant across the whole nightly batch — it already is here since every call forces the same tool.
5. **`Promise.all` over the per-persona sampling loop aborts the whole night on one bad session.** If session A's judge call throws and B/C are awaited in the same `Promise.all`, the entire batch rejects and nothing after A gets scored. Use `Promise.allSettled` and log+skip individual failures (see 4b.2).
6. **Do not silently insert a partial/failed score to satisfy idempotency.** The `by_idempotencyKey` early-return guard (Phase 88 precedent) must key on session+date, not on "attempt made" — a session whose judge call fails all retries should have *no* row, so it can be legitimately re-sampled another night, not a permanently poisoned dedup key.

## 4. Implementation Guidance

**Model Configuration:**
- **Primary (default):** `claude-haiku-4-5` via Anthropic Messages API. `max_tokens: 1024`, `temperature: 0` (deterministic judging — this is scoring, not creative generation), `tool_choice: {"type": "tool", "name": "score_session"}`. No `budget_tokens`/extended-thinking config — unsupported on Opus 4.8+/Claude 5 SDKs and incompatible with forced tool_choice regardless.
- **Fallback:** OpenAI-compatible model (e.g. `gpt-4o-mini`) via `response_format: {"type": "json_schema", "json_schema": {"name": "score_session", "strict": true, "schema": <same shape as JUDGE_TOOL.input_schema, with nullable unions for any field not always populated>}}`.
- **Config slot:** stored under `agentConfigs` key `intelligence.llm_eval` (a *third* slot alongside `llm_primary`/`llm_backup`, not reusing either — D-07). Same `{provider, model, apiKey}` shape and `setLLMConfig`-style mutation, extended to accept `"eval"` as a valid `slot` value.

**Core Pattern:**
```typescript
// convex/evalScores.ts (sketch)

async function callJudgeLLM(
  runQuery: (fn: any, args: any) => Promise<any>,
  systemPrompt: string,
  userPrompt: string
): Promise<Record<string, unknown>> {
  const config = await runQuery(internal.evalScores.getEvalLLMConfigInternal, {});
  if (!config?.apiKey) throw new Error("Eval LLM not configured (intelligence.llm_eval)");

  const MAX_ATTEMPTS = 3; // 1 initial + 2 repair retries — see 4b.1
  let lastError: unknown;

  for (let attempt = 1; attempt <= MAX_ATTEMPTS; attempt++) {
    try {
      const raw =
        config.provider === "anthropic"
          ? await callAnthropicJudge(config.apiKey, config.model, systemPrompt, userPrompt)
          : await callOpenAIJudge(config.apiKey, config.model, systemPrompt, userPrompt);

      const parsed = JudgeOutputSchema.safeParse(raw); // zod, see 4b.1
      if (parsed.success) return parsed.data;

      // Validation failed on well-formed JSON that doesn't match the rubric shape.
      console.error(
        `[eval-judge] schema validation failed, attempt ${attempt}: ${parsed.error.message}`
      );
      lastError = parsed.error;
      // Repair pass: append the validation error to the next attempt's user prompt.
      userPrompt += `\n\nYour previous response failed validation: ${parsed.error.message}. Return corrected values.`;
    } catch (err) {
      console.error(`[eval-judge] call failed, attempt ${attempt}: ${String(err)}`);
      lastError = err;
    }
  }
  throw new Error(`Judge failed after ${MAX_ATTEMPTS} attempts: ${String(lastError)}`);
}
```

**Tool Use:**
- Single forced tool (`score_session`) per call, defined once in code (`JUDGE_TOOL` in Section 3) and reused for both the Anthropic branch (as `tools[0].input_schema`) and the OpenAI branch (translated into `response_format.json_schema.schema`) — one source of truth for the rubric shape, not two schemas drifting independently.
- No other tools are given to the judge model — it never calls out to fetch data itself; all session context is pre-assembled by Convex queries and handed to it as plain text in the user prompt (see Context Window Strategy below). This keeps the call a single request/response, not an agentic loop.

**State Management:**
- Digest input (session/events/llmMetrics) is read via `internalQuery` — no writes on that path.
- Judge output is written via a dedicated `internalMutation` (`storeEvalScore`) that takes `sessionId`, `personaId`, `scoreName: "llm_judge"`, per-dimension scores + rationales, `overall`, and an `idempotencyKey` (e.g. `` `judge:${sessionId}` ``). Follows the Phase 88 `idempotencyKey` + `by_idempotencyKey` index early-return precedent: look up the key first, no-op if present, insert if not — one `internalMutation` call, no separate read-then-write race across two functions.
- Ástríðr's own binary `task_quality` mirror (EVAL-01) inserts into the *same* `evalScores` table with `scoreName: "task_quality"` and its own idempotency key derived from the producer's event id — the KPI queries in Section 4b.4/Section 5 read both `scoreName`s from one table, not two parallel schemas.

**Context Window Strategy:**
- No transcripts are ever fetched (D-06 hard constraint — no localhost reachability from the Convex cloud action). The entire "context window management" problem here is **digest construction**, not chunking/retrieval:
  - Cap `events` read to `.take(200)` per session (mirrors `getSessionDataInternal` in `briefings.ts`).
  - Aggregate, don't enumerate: group by `toolName`/`eventType` into counts (reuse `groupActivityEvents` shape), not a per-event dump.
  - Truncate any free-text event field (error messages, tool output snippets) to ~200–300 chars before inclusion — a single verbose stack trace should not dominate the prompt.
  - Pull `llmMetrics` as summary stats (total cost, call count, token totals) for the session, not per-call rows.
  - Target digest size: well under 1–2K tokens per session. `claude-haiku-4-5`'s 200K context window is never the binding constraint here — cost and judge-attention quality are (a shorter, denser digest scores more reliably than a long noisy one).

### 4b.1 Structured Outputs — zod as the TypeScript equivalent of Pydantic

There is no Pydantic in a Convex `internalAction` (it's a V8 isolate running TypeScript, not Python). The direct equivalent for "define the schema, validate or retry" is **zod**, paired with the forced-tool-use mechanism from Section 3/4 that gets the model to emit schema-shaped JSON in the first place. Two layers, both needed:

1. **Model-side shaping** — `tool_choice`-forced tool call (Anthropic) or `response_format.json_schema` with `strict: true` (OpenAI fallback). This makes malformed JSON rare, not impossible.
2. **Code-side validation** — zod re-validates the parsed object against the same shape, because "the model returned syntactically valid JSON matching the schema" (guaranteed by strict/forced modes) is not the same guarantee as "the *values* are sane" (e.g. a model can legally emit `task_completion: 1` for every session even inside a valid schema — zod won't catch that, but a numeric `.min(0).max(1)` bound plus downstream regression-detection sanity checks are the backstop).

```typescript
import { z } from "zod";

const JudgeOutputSchema = z.object({
  task_completion: z.number().min(0).max(1),
  task_completion_rationale: z.string().min(1).max(500),
  error_handling: z.number().min(0).max(1),
  error_handling_rationale: z.string().min(1).max(500),
  tool_efficiency: z.number().min(0).max(1),
  tool_efficiency_rationale: z.string().min(1).max(500),
  cost_discipline: z.number().min(0).max(1),
  cost_discipline_rationale: z.string().min(1).max(500),
  overall: z.number().min(0).max(1),
});
type JudgeOutput = z.infer<typeof JudgeOutputSchema>;
```

**Framework integration:** since this is raw `fetch()` (no LangChain `.with_structured_output()`, no LlamaIndex `PydanticOutputParser`, no `instructor`), the pattern is: forced tool_choice/strict `response_format` → `JSON.parse` is not even needed for the Anthropic branch (the SDK-shape `tool_use.input` field arrives already parsed as a JS object from `resp.json()`) → `JudgeOutputSchema.safeParse(raw)`.

**Retry logic:** 3 attempts total (1 initial + 2 repairs), matching the `callJudgeLLM` sketch in Section 4:
- On a zod validation failure (well-formed JSON, wrong values/shape), append the zod error message to the *next* attempt's user prompt as a repair instruction ("Your previous response failed validation: ... Return corrected values.").
- On a transport/HTTP failure (429, 5xx, timeout), retry without a repair message — the prompt was fine, the call itself failed.
- Log every failed attempt with `sessionId`, attempt number, and the zod error or HTTP status — never log the API key (matches the existing `T-07-05` apiKey-redaction discipline in `briefings.ts`).
- On exhausting all 3 attempts: do **not** insert a partial or zeroed `evalScores` row. Log a single `console.error` summarizing the sessionId and reason, and let that session simply go unscored for the night (it remains eligible for a future night's sample — no idempotency key is written for a failed attempt, so nothing is permanently blocked). Surface via the nightly job's own summary/count, not a per-session alert — a single judge miss is not page-worthy; a *pattern* of misses (e.g. the eval provider is down) would surface as "zero scores landed tonight," which is a fair candidate for a low-severity alert if it recurs.

### 4b.2 Async-First Design

Convex `internalAction`/`internalQuery`/`internalMutation` handlers are always `async (ctx, args) => {...}` — there is no sync/async split to choose between (unlike Python's `asyncio.run()`-in-a-running-loop trap). The Node/V8 event loop model means every `await` is safe to use directly; there's no equivalent footgun to "don't call `asyncio.run()` inside an already-running loop."

**The one common mistake in *this* codebase's shape:** running the per-persona sampling loop with `Promise.all(sessions.map(judgeOne))`. If any single session's judge call throws after exhausting its 3 attempts, `Promise.all` rejects immediately and **every other in-flight session in that batch is abandoned** — a single flaky session silently zeroes out the whole night's KPI data for every persona. Use `Promise.allSettled` and iterate the results, logging/skipping individual rejections:

```typescript
const results = await Promise.allSettled(sampledSessions.map((s) => judgeOneSession(ctx, s)));
const failures = results.filter((r) => r.status === "rejected");
if (failures.length > 0) {
  console.error(`[eval-judge] ${failures.length}/${results.length} sessions failed judging tonight`);
}
```

**Stream vs. await:** always `await` here, never stream. Streaming exists to improve perceived UX for long free-text generation displayed token-by-token to a user; the judge call produces a small structured object that must be fully parsed and zod-validated before it's meaningful at all — there is no partial-JSON UI to render mid-stream, and Anthropic's forced-tool-use response arrives as one atomic `tool_use` block regardless of streaming. This contrasts with `briefings.ts`'s narrative generation, which *could* stream to a UI (it currently doesn't) since it's free text meant for human reading, not a value to validate.

### 4b.3 Prompt Engineering Discipline

- **System vs. user separation:** the system prompt holds the fixed rubric definition (4 dimensions + overall, scoring scale, "judge observable behavior only, no chain-of-thought needed since output is forced via tool call") and is identical across every call in a given night — this is exactly the static/dynamic split that makes it a good prompt-caching candidate (4b.5). The user prompt holds only the per-session digest (counts, durations, cost, truncated error snippets) — never a chat history, since there is no multi-turn conversation with the judge.
- **Few-shot:** inline, not dynamically retrieved. At 3–4 rubric dimensions and a fixed scoring scale, 1–2 short anchor examples embedded directly in the system prompt (one clearly-good session digest → scores, one clearly-bad one → scores) are enough to calibrate the model; there's no retrieval corpus of past judgments to draw from in v1 (rubric-editing/calibration UI is explicitly deferred).
- **`max_tokens` is always explicit and bounded:** `1024` for the judge call. Four dimension scores + four short rationales (capped at ~500 chars each in the zod schema) + an overall score comfortably fits; forcing `tool_choice` also means no preamble/chatter tokens are burned before the structured block, unlike an `auto` tool_choice call where the model might narrate first.

### 4b.4 Context Window Management

This is an **Extraction** system judging **observable behavior only** (D-06) — there is no RAG corpus to chunk/rerank and no multi-turn conversation to summarize, so "context window management" reduces entirely to **digest construction discipline**, covered concretely in Section 4's Context Window Strategy: cap events to `.take(200)`, aggregate into tool/eventType counts rather than enumerating, truncate free-text fields to ~200–300 chars, and pull `llmMetrics` as summary stats. The judge call is stateless and independent per session — nothing carries forward between sessions or nights, so there is no session-to-session compaction/summarization concern the way there would be in a Conversational or Autonomous system. If the deferred "session-transcript mirroring" capability is ever added (see 93-CONTEXT.md Deferred Ideas), that would introduce a real chunking/summarization problem for the first time and should get its own Section 4b.4 revision at that point — don't pre-build truncation logic for transcripts that don't exist yet.

### 4b.5 Cost and Latency Budget

**Per-call cost (illustrative — verify current Anthropic pricing before finalizing):** `claude-haiku-4-5` is priced at roughly $1/M input tokens, $5/M output tokens. A digest-built judge call is small: ~1,200–1,800 input tokens (system rubric + few-shot anchors + one session digest) and ~300–500 output tokens (forced tool call, no preamble).

- Per call: ~1,500 in × $1/M + ~400 out × $5/M ≈ **$0.0035/call**.
- At D-08's sampling rate (~3 sessions/persona/night), an 8-persona system runs ~24 judge calls/night ≈ **$0.08/night ≈ $2.50/month**. Even at 20 personas (~60 calls/night) this stays under $10/month — the sampling cap (not the model choice) is what bounds cost, and it already scales with persona count by design.
- **Caching:** the system prompt (rubric + scoring instructions + few-shot anchors) is byte-identical across every call in a night and easily exceeds the 1,024-token prompt-cache minimum — add a `cache_control` breakpoint on that system block to cut its repeated cost by ~90% on cache hits (all but the first call each night). **Exact-match response caching is not applicable** here (every session digest is unique, so there's nothing to hit on a second call) and semantic caching would be actively wrong for a judge — two similar-looking sessions should still get independently judged, not have a cached score reused.
- **Sub-task model routing:** there is no cheaper sub-task to route out of this call — `claude-haiku-4-5` is already the cheap end of the model lineup, and the entire call *is* the sub-task (classification-shaped: bounded numeric outputs from a fixed rubric). A deferred, optional future lever: a cheap deterministic pre-filter (e.g., skip LLM judging entirely for a session with zero errors and zero tool calls, since there's nothing for the judge to differentiate) could shave a fraction of calls, but this is not required to hit the cost target above and isn't part of this phase's scope.

## 5. Evaluation Strategy

> This system **is** an eval pipeline, so Section 5 evaluates the *evaluator*, not a downstream product. The dimensions below test the machinery that produces scores — persistence, parse-reliability, calibration, and alert precision — because a wrong judge score or a dropped row is this phase's actual failure surface. Code-based checks (convex-test + zod invariants) come first; the LLM judge itself is only spot-checked by the one human in the loop.

### Dimensions

| # | Dimension (grounded in Failure Mode) | Rubric — PASS / FAIL | Measurement | Priority |
|---|--------------------------------------|----------------------|-------------|----------|
| E1 | **Ingest exactly-once persistence** (FM1 silent loss, D-05) | PASS: a redelivered `task_quality` event (same producer id / session+date key) yields exactly one `evalScores` row; a judge call that exhausts all 3 retries yields **zero** rows and leaves the session re-sampleable. FAIL: duplicate insert on retry, a poisoned idempotency key that permanently blocks re-sampling, or any partial/zeroed row written on judge failure. | Code (convex-test: dedup early-return test + "no row on exhausted retries" invariant test) | Critical |
| E2 | **Structured-output parse reliability** (FM4, 4b.1) | PASS: every judge response either passes `JudgeOutputSchema.safeParse` (all dims numeric in [0,1], each rationale non-empty ≤500 chars) within 3 attempts, or is dropped with a logged reason and no row. FAIL: an out-of-range score, missing dimension, or empty rationale reaches `evalScores`; or a malformed response throws and aborts the batch (`Promise.all` instead of `allSettled`). | Code (zod bound tests + fixture test replaying recorded judge outputs, including malformed/out-of-range ones — no live LLM) | Critical |
| E3 | **Judge calibration vs human** (FM2, the highest-likelihood failure) | PASS: on the reference set, judge `overall` agrees with Larry's hand labels at **≥0.7** agreement/rank-correlation, and each rationale cites evidence actually present in that session's digest. FAIL: the judge is trusted/acted-on with **zero** spot-check ever done; agreement <0.7 yet trends are still read as truth; or scores collapse into a narrow band (e.g. everything 0.7–0.8) with no discriminating power. | Human (Larry spot-checks) + LLM Judge output; recurring monthly, and once before first trusting the Quality page | Critical |
| E4 | **Regression-detector precision** (FM3 crying-wolf, D-14) | PASS: an alert fires **only** when ≥5 judged sessions per side AND the before/after `overall` mean drop clears the code-defined threshold; a 2-vs-2, a 4-vs-6, a sub-threshold drift, or a single-outlier swing never fires. FAIL: fires below the min-sample gate or on ordinary night-to-night variance, training Larry to mute the channel. | Code (convex-test with synthetic before/after fixtures at the boundary: 4-vs-6 sample sizes, just-under-threshold drops, single-bad-session outliers) | Critical |
| E5 | **Trend attributability / version stamping** (FM5 poisoned trend) | PASS: every judge `evalScores` row carries a rubric-version + judge model/config-slot marker, and a KPI query can distinguish scores generated under different rulers. FAIL: a rubric-wording or `intelligence.llm_eval` model change leaves no marker, splicing two measurement instruments into one continuous line. | Code (schema-presence test on the version field + query test proving a version change is visible in results) | High |
| E6 | **Digest fidelity** (FM4, D-06 evidence-preservation) | PASS: for the error-heavy / tool-churn reference sessions, the truncated digest still names the failing tool/operation that drove the bad outcome, and the judge rationale references it. FAIL: a badly-failed session scores "fine" because the deciding error was aggregated or truncated out of the digest. | Human (spot-check the digest text produced for the error-heavy reference sessions — did the signal survive truncation?) | Medium |
| E7 | **Cron completion / liveness** (FM1 silent breakage) | PASS: the nightly judge action runs on its offset UTC slot and logs a summary (`N sampled / N scored / N failed`); a night where zero scores land is detectable from that summary. FAIL: the cron silently skips or fails with no summary, making "no data" indistinguishable from "nothing happened." | Code (assert the job emits a summary count; a recurring-zero-scores condition is alert-eligible — see Section 7) | High |

**Mandatory live completion bar (D-04, not a test-suite claim):** the phase is not evaluated as done until a **real** Ástríðr-emitted `task_quality` score travels the full path — `spawn_score()` mirror POST → prod Convex `tidy-whale-981` `/runtime-ingest` → `evalScores` row → rendered on the Quality page. Green convex-test alone does **not** close this (Phase 90 lesson: the cross-repo gate was flagged but only live testing surfaced the real gaps). This is a manual verification step in the plan.

### Eval Tooling

**Primary Tool:** **vitest + convex-test** (already in the repo — `src/**/*.test.tsx`, `convex/**/*.test.ts`, jsdom setup at `src/test/setup.ts`). Plus **zod** as the runtime invariant layer (4b.1). Judge-prompt regression is a **fixture-based test** replaying *recorded* judge outputs — no live-LLM assertion in CI.

> **Phoenix override (explicit):** the workflow default of *Arize Phoenix as the tracing/eval default* is **overridden** for this phase. Self-hosting Phoenix or Langfuse is **out of scope** (REQUIREMENTS.md); CodePulse itself is the observability surface, and Ástríðr already runs Langfuse on the producer side (T-73-16). Prompt-regression coverage is therefore recorded-fixture replay under vitest, not Promptfoo hitting a real model.

### Reference Dataset

**Size:** **10–20 real Ástríðr sessions** pulled from prod Convex (`tidy-whale-981`). Start at 10 high-quality labeled sessions; expand toward 20 as real failure modes accrue (per ai-evals.md — 10–20 good beats 200 mediocre).

**Composition (deliberately spans the failure surface, not random):**
- **Clean completions** — low-error, efficient sessions (calibration high anchor; guards against a judge that scores everything low).
- **Error-heavy sessions** — retries, failed tool calls, exceptions (feeds E6 digest-fidelity: did the failing tool survive truncation?).
- **Tool-churn sessions** — high tool-call counts, thrashing (tests the `tool_efficiency` dimension's discrimination).
- **Expensive / high-cost sessions** — large `llmMetrics` cost/token totals (tests `cost_discipline`).
- At least one **boundary pair per E4 fixture** — sessions arranged so a before/after window lands at 4-vs-6 samples and at a just-under-threshold drop, to prove the detector *doesn't* fire.

**Labeling:** **Larry, the sole domain expert (Section 1b).** He hand-labels `overall` + the four per-dimension scores by reading each session. These labels do double duty: (1) the calibration gold set for E3 (≥0.7 agreement gate before the Quality page's trends are trusted), and (2) frozen digest/score **fixtures** for the E2/E4/E5 code tests. No second labeler exists at this scale — stated honestly, not invented.

**Timeline:** Build **during** implementation, not after — pull and label the sessions as soon as the digest builder exists, so the calibration gate and the CI fixtures are ready before the Quality page is trusted or the phase is closed.

## 6. Guardrails

> Guardrails are deliberately minimal and mostly code-cheap — this pipeline runs nightly for one operator, so the priority is *never silently corrupting or losing a score*, not blocking a user in real time. "Online" here means "on every ingest / judge write," not "on a user request."

### Online (Real-Time)

| Guardrail | Trigger | Intervention |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| Ingest auth + schema validation | Every `/runtime-ingest` `task_quality` POST | **Block** — reject non-Bearer requests (`validateIngestAuth`) and any payload failing `v.` validators; inherits the existing gate, no new route |
| Score-range clamp/reject | A `task_quality` or judge dimension/overall score outside `[0,1]` | **Block** — reject the row (zod `.min(0).max(1)` on the judge path; `v.` bound on ingest); an out-of-range value never persists |
| Idempotency dedup | A redelivered event (same producer id) or re-run judge (same session+date key) | **Block duplicate** — `by_idempotencyKey` early-return no-op; exactly one row (E1) |
| Judge-output zod rejection | Judge response fails `JudgeOutputSchema` after all 3 attempts | **Flag** — log `sessionId` + reason, write **no row** (session stays re-sampleable); never insert a partial/zeroed score to satisfy dedup |
| Alert min-sample gate | A regression comparison with <5 judged sessions on either side | **Block alert** — suppress firing until both windows clear the D-14 min-sample floor (the core crying-wolf guard) |

### Offline (Flywheel)

| Metric | Sampling Strategy | Action on Degradation |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| Nightly judge batch | Up to ~3 completed sessions per active persona per night, random within the day (D-08) | Feeds per-persona KPI trends; the batch's own `N sampled / N scored / N failed` summary is the primary silent-breakage signal |
| Monthly judge calibration spot-check | Larry reads a handful of judge rationales against the actual sessions (weighted by the divergence filter in Section 7) | If human agreement drops **<0.7**, revise rubric wording / few-shot anchors or stop trusting the trend line until re-calibrated (E3) |
| Rubric / judge-version stamping | Every judge `evalScores` row carries rubric-version + judge model/config marker | On any version bump, treat pre/post scores as **separate series** — never splice across a ruler change (E5) |
| Zero-scores-landed watch | Nightly, from the batch summary; recurring-miss detection across consecutive nights | If **zero** scores land for ≥2 consecutive nights (provider down / cron broken), raise a low-severity alert — a *pattern* of misses, not a single one, is page-worthy (4b.1) |

## 7. Production Monitoring

**Tracing Tool:** **CodePulse's own tables (`evalScores`, `llmMetrics`) + the existing alert engine (`convex/alerts.ts`)** — the Quality page is the human-facing trace view; judge `llmMetrics` rows are the cost/latency trace.

> **Phoenix override (explicit, restated for this section):** the workflow default of *Arize Phoenix as the tracing default* does **not** apply here. Self-hosting Phoenix/Langfuse is out of scope (REQUIREMENTS.md); **CodePulse is itself the observability product**, so its own Convex tables + alert engine *are* the tracing/monitoring surface, and Ástríðr already runs Langfuse on the producer side. No external tracing platform is added.

**Key Metrics to Track:**
1. **Per-persona overall-score trend** (30-day default, range picker per D-17) with delta badge — the core quality signal; a downward trend adjacent to a change-event marker is the whole point of the phase.
2. **Judged-sessions-per-night count** — liveness / silent-breakage signal (E7); "zero scores landed" must be visible, not indistinguishable from "quiet night."
3. **Judge call cost / tokens** (from `llmMetrics`) vs. the ~$2.50/mo budget (4b.5) — cost drift / runaway-sampling guard.
4. **Regression alerts fired vs. muted** — the precision gauge; a rising *mute rate* is the early warning that E4/D-14 thresholds are crying wolf and need re-tuning by commit.
5. **`task_quality` ingest freshness / dropped-row signal** — EVAL-01's core loss guard; scores arriving from Ástríðr and persisting exactly once.

**Alert Thresholds:**
- **Regression:** before/after `overall` mean drop **≥ the code-defined threshold** AND **≥5 judged sessions per side** → raise through the existing alert engine (inherits Discord/Slack/PagerDuty/email routing, severity prefs, ack/mute lifecycle — D-13). Thresholds/windows are code constants, tuned by commit (D-14), no settings UI this phase.
- **Silent-breakage:** **zero** judge scores landed for **≥2 consecutive nights** → low-severity alert (eval provider down or cron broken).
- **No per-session alert:** a single judge miss is expected noise and is *not* page-worthy (4b.1) — only patterns escalate.

**Smart Sampling Strategy (what Larry reviews by hand):**
Weight the scarce human-review budget toward **signal-metric divergence**, the ai-evals.md early-warning filter:
- Sessions where the **LLM judge `overall` diverges sharply from Ástríðr's binary `task_quality`** on the *same* session (judge says fine / producer says fail, or vice versa) — the single strongest "the judge may be miscalibrated or the digest missed something" trigger.
- Sessions **adjacent to a change-event marker** (`profileSwitches` / persona-scoped `configChanges`) that opened a regression comparison window — confirm the drop is real before trusting the alert.
- **Low-overall + high-cost** sessions — most likely to reveal a real regression worth acting on, and the highest-value calibration checks for E3/E6.
